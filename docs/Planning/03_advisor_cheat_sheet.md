## **Medical Recheck Planning**

We are confirmed to complete medical recheck during Stay & Play on Monday, June 15\. This is not a courtesy check. Philmont will deny backcountry access to anyone who does not meet their standards. Identify every potential issue before travel. There is no fixing a weight, blood pressure, or medication problem at the gate.

### **Standards Applied to All Participants**

**BMI / Height-Weight:**  
 Maximum BMI of 40 for participants under 21\. Maximum BMI of 32 for participants 21 and older. Anyone over the limit will not be allowed on trail and may be sent home at their own expense. Verify compliance before departure.\[^8\] Do not bring someone to Philmont who is over the limit and expect to negotiate.

**Blood Pressure:**  
 Philmont requires a reading below 160/100 for all participants before going on trail. This applies to youth and adults. Anyone with hypertension or a cardiovascular history must be controlled and below threshold at recheck. Participants on blood pressure medication must be taking it consistently before arrival.

Altitude increases blood pressure above home baseline readings. Participants on BP medication need to be especially attentive to dosing and hydration before and during the trek. Dehydration also elevates blood pressure. Both advisors and the participant share responsibility for managing this on trail.

**Medications:**  
 All prescription medications must be in original labeled containers. No exceptions. Emergency medications — EpiPens, inhalers, and any other life-safety medication — must be present, in date, and sufficient for the full trek. Expired medication means denied access until replaced at the participant's expense.

**Medical Forms:**  
 Annual Health and Medical Record must be complete, correctly signed, and uploaded to Philmont Gateway before arrival. Missing form, missing signature, or missing required physician sign-off is a hold on backcountry access until resolved.

### **If Someone Does Not Pass Medical Recheck**

* Denied means denied. Work directly with Philmont medical staff. Do not try to work around them and do not promise a reversal.  
* Keep the rest of the crew calm and on schedule. One person's issue is not the whole crew's problem.  
* Escalate to parent or guardian contact immediately using the pre-trip contact plan. Have that plan ready before you leave home.

### **Advisor Checklist Before Travel**

* \[ \] Every Annual Health and Medical Record complete and signed.

## **Activity Timing and Risk Notes**

The main risk on heavy program days is a cascade failure:

1. Late wake-up or slow camp breakdown.  
2. Slow hiking pace or too many breaks.  
3. Arriving at program camp too late.  
4. Getting pushed behind other crews.  
5. Afternoon weather cancels or delays the activity.  
6. Last crew in line gets a shortened version or misses the program entirely.

Planning standard:

* Arrive at program camps and activity areas by lunch whenever possible.  
* Be one of the first crews ready to participate.  
* The Crew Leader knows each day's priority before leaving camp.

**Break discipline:**  
 Breaks are either 2 minutes or 20 minutes. Decide before stopping which one it is and say it out loud. A 2-minute break is a standing rest — water, snack, no packs off. A 20-minute break is packs off, real rest, real food, full stop. Set a timer when the break starts. On a 20-minute break, call a 2-minute warning so everyone has packs on and is ready to move when time is up. The break ends when the Crew Leader says it ends, not when the last person decides they feel like standing up. Whoever calls the break owns the restart.

**Caterpillar hiking technique:**  
 A rolling rest for steep ascents. Use it when the crew starts to struggle or spread out. When the lead hiker starts to struggle, they step to the side of the trail. The second hiker steps up and becomes the new leader, hikes 20–30 feet, then also steps aside. The rotation continues down the line. When the last person passes the original leader, that hiker rejoins at the back and the cycle repeats.

Why it works: stopping and restarting builds lactic acid and costs more energy than continuous slow movement. The caterpillar gives each hiker a standing rest while the line passes, keeps the crew condensed on hard terrain, and prevents anyone from getting dropped or separated on a climb.

Video: Caterpillar Hiking — Scouting America, Patriots' Path Council (YouTube, Nov 2022): https://share.google/aimode/apQAyZpgJ1LbQzzhd

**Daily crew brief:**  
 The Crew Leader briefs the full crew each night at dinner about the next day. This covers: route, distance and elevation, water sources, program timing, conservation or food pickup requirements, and the wake-up target. Before that brief, the Crew Leader, Lead Advisor, and Navigator meet to review the map, confirm the route, and identify navigation decision points. Questions get answered in private first. The crew hears a clear plan, not a debate.

High-risk days for this pattern on Trek 12-23:

* **Day 7 (June 22):** Burro pickup at Miranda, then 7.3 miles to Pueblano with programs.  
* **Day 8 (June 23):** Conservation at Sioux is at 2:00 PM. A late start puts the whole day at risk.  
* **Day 9 (June 24):** 8.1 miles to Dean Cow for rock climbing. Late arrival cuts program time.  
* **Day 10 (June 25):** 10.0 miles. Harlan shotgun pass-through can delay if not handled efficiently.

## **Crew Development Phases**

Use this as a leadership lens. Exact timing varies by crew.

### **Forming — Before Philmont through Trail Day 2**

The crew is excited, polite, unsure, and looking for direction.

What it looks like: Scouts are cooperative but not yet operating independently. They look to the Ranger and advisors for answers. Roles are not yet instinctive.

**Advisor standard during Forming:** When a Scout asks an advisor a question the Crew Leader should answer, the standard response is: "Ask your Crew Leader." This is deliberate, not dismissive. Scouts must learn that the Crew Leader is their point of contact for decisions, direction, and problem-solving. Advisors work through the Crew Leader, the Chaplain's Aide, and the Wilderness Pledge Guía. This gives those youth leaders the opportunity to own their roles from the start, not have advisors step in front of them.

Advisor and Crew Leader focus:

* Set expectations early. Make the crew structure explicit before the trek starts.  
* Clarify crew jobs and duty roster.  
* Build confidence without pretending the trek will be easy.  
* Let the Crew Leader lead small moments before trail day.  
* Use the Ranger as a coach, not a crutch.  
* Redirect questions and decisions to youth leaders consistently.

### **Storming — Trail Days 2–4 (approx. June 18–20)**

Fatigue, chores, hunger, altitude, pack weight, and personality differences surface. For Trek 12-23, the likely storming window is Cimarron River, Santa Claus, Head of Dean, and the start of Black Horse Creek.

What it looks like: Arguments about chores. Scouts questioning Crew Leader decisions. Advisors being approached with complaints. Camp taking too long. Pace problems.

Advisor and Crew Leader focus:

* Keep conflict small and early.  
* Correct behaviors, not personalities.  
* Hold the duty roster. Do not let it slip.  
* Keep the crew together on trail.  
* Protect the Crew Leader from being bypassed.  
* Debrief briefly at night: what worked, what failed, what changes tomorrow.  
* Advisors stay in the advising role. Do not step in and take over from the Crew Leader except for safety issues.

### **Norming — Trail Days 5–8 (approx. June 20–23)**

Systems start working. Scouts begin to know their jobs. The crew gets faster in the morning and calmer at night.

What it looks like: Wake-up is smoother. Camp breakdown time drops. The crew starts helping before being asked. Crew Leader gets fewer challenges.

Advisor and Crew Leader focus:

* Reinforce what is working specifically.  
* Keep standards high before Baldy, burro, and conservation days.  
* Push ownership back to youth leaders.  
* Avoid advisor over-functioning.

### **Performing — Trail Days 9–11 (approx. June 24–27)**

The crew should be moving with purpose. Jobs are understood. Scouts help before being asked. Crew Leader can focus on route, pace, safety, and timing.

What it looks like: Early wake-up without drama. Fast camp breakdown. Crew looks out for each other. Crew Leader is clearly in command. Advisors are mostly watching.

Advisor and Crew Leader focus:

* Watch for overconfidence on the last two days.  
* Protect weather windows on Tooth day.  
* Keep nutrition and hydration visible.  
* Maintain early starts. Tooth day is the hardest day after Baldy.

### **Adjourning — Day 12 and Departure (June 27–28)**

The crew finishes, processes the experience, and heads home.

What it looks like: Emotional, tired, proud. Some Scouts may become reflective or quiet. Some may crash after Base Camp arrival.

Advisor and Crew Leader focus:

* Celebrate without losing safety discipline.  
* Keep everyone together through Base Camp and travel.  
* Capture lessons while they are fresh.  
* Thank the Crew Leader and youth leaders specifically.  
* The trip is not over until everyone is home safely.

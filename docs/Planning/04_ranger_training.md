# Ranger Training Report — Trek 12-23

**Audience:** Scouts, advisors, Crew Leader, youth leaders  
 **Purpose:** Explain what the Ranger will teach and what the crew must be able to do before the Ranger leaves.

---

## **Short Overview**

The Ranger is not there to carry the crew. The Ranger is there to train the crew so we can safely run our own trek.

The Guidebook to Adventure explains: "The Ranger's job is to lead you through the Philmont check-in and to accompany you for two days on the trail to coach and review camping and hiking skills."\[^9\]

The Ranger will leave when the crew has shown reasonable proficiency. From that point, the Crew Leader leads and the crew owns the trek.

---

## **When This Happens on Trek 12-23**

* **June 16, Day 1:** Ranger guides Base Camp check-in, gear shakedown, logistics review, and Day 1 prep.  
* **June 17, Trail Day 1:** Ranger Training begins at Cimarron River. The primary systems day.  
* **June 18, Trail Day 2:** Ranger continues coaching while the crew hikes to Santa Claus.  
* **June 19, Trail Day 3:** Ranger will likely leave early if the crew has shown reasonable skill. Crew Leader takes command.

---

## **Detailed Ranger Training Topics**

### **1\. Crew Leadership and Patrol Method**

The Ranger expects the Crew Leader to lead and the crew to work through assigned jobs. Philmont uses the Patrol Method.

Key crew roles:

* Crew Leader  
* Chaplain's Aide  
* Wilderness Pledge Guía  
* Navigator  
* Cook  
* Dishwasher  
* Bear bags manager  
* Water gatherer  
* Fire guard (if fires are permitted)

What success looks like: Scouts do the work. The Crew Leader gives direction. Advisors advise. Jobs are done without drama. Nobody waits around while a few people work.

---

### **2\. Map and Compass Navigation**

The Ranger checks that the crew can use maps, compass, route descriptions, and the Crew Leader copy.

Expect to learn or review:

* Orienting the map.  
* Reading contour lines.  
* Understanding trail junctions.  
* Confirming the route before leaving camp.  
* Stopping when confused instead of guessing.  
* Keeping the crew together.

Official video: How To Use A Map & Compass — https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VIh43ViXVY8

---

### **3\. Hiking Etiquette and Pace**

The Ranger teaches the crew how to move together.

Expect:

* Single file on trail.  
* Crew stays together. No one hikes alone. "Rule of four" applies.  
* Pace is set by the crew, not the fastest Scout.  
* Short, controlled breaks.  
* Clear communication from front to back.

For Trek 12-23: pacing matters because late starts and slow breaks cost program time and weather windows.

---

### **4\. Lightning and Weather Safety**

The Guidebook to Adventure covers lightning risk in detail: "Reduce lightning risk in the backcountry by timing your visits to high-risk areas with local weather patterns, find safer terrain if you hear thunder, avoid trees and long conductors once lightning gets close."\[^10\]

Expect the Ranger to cover:

* Watch clouds and thunderheads.  
* Avoid exposed ridges and peaks when storms build.  
* Move through alpine areas before noon when possible.  
* Lightning position: feet together, crouching, crew spread 30 feet apart.  
* Leave high or exposed areas quickly when lightning risk rises.

Trek 12-23 crew rules:

* Off Baldy summit by 11:00 AM, no later than 12:00.  
* Off the Tooth summit and exposed ridge by 11:00 AM, no later than 12:00.  
* Turn around if weather makes the summit unsafe.

Official weather resource: https://www.philmontscoutranch.org/resources/preparing-for-philmont/weather-climates/

---

### **5\. Emergency Procedures and First Aid**

The Guidebook states: "Philmont requires that at least three persons in each crew be currently certified in Wilderness First Aid and CPR."\[^11\]

The Ranger will review:

* Keep the crew together.  
* Use first aid kit for minor issues.  
* Notify advisors and Crew Leader early.  
* Use staffed camps and radios for serious problems.  
* Rule of four: if someone needs help, one treats the injured, two go for help, one stays.

The most common injuries treated at the Philmont Infirmary are sprains, blisters, altitude sickness, dehydration, upper respiratory infections, and gastrointestinal issues.\[^12\]

---

### **6\. Wildfire Safety**

Expect:

* Follow all fire restrictions.  
* Use established fire rings only when allowed.  
* Do not toss matches. Hold the match until it stops smoking, break it, step on it.  
* Never leave flame unattended.  
* Report smoke or suspected fire to the nearest staffed camp.  
* Do not try to fight a wildfire as a crew.

---

### **7\. Backcountry Hygiene**

The Ranger will teach hygiene because one sick crew member can affect everyone.

Expect:

* Wash or sanitize hands before food handling.  
* Proper bathroom practices — catholes at least 200 feet from trail, water, and campsites.  
* Keep personal and crew dishes clean.  
* Keep smellables organized and controlled.

---

### **8\. Campsite Setup and Bearmuda Triangle**

The Ranger will show how to set up camp using the Bearmuda Triangle method. The Guidebook to Adventure explains: "This method concentrates all activities that might have a smell within a triangle formed by the fire ring, sump and bear cable (or bear box). Tents are set up outside the triangle while backpacks, dining fly, cooking, and dish washing are set up inside the triangle."\[^13\]

Key zones:

* Tent area (outside the triangle, at least 50 feet away).  
* Cooking area near the fire ring.  
* Sump location.  
* Bear cable location.  
* Water source.

Official video: How To Set Up A Campsite — https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BPnwAUhQjMA

---

### **9\. Water Purification and Water Planning**

Expect:

* All water from all sources — including springs, streams, and wells — must be purified.  
* Most staffed camps have treated water. Confirm with staff.  
* Philmont provides Micropur tablets (chlorine dioxide) as the standard treatment.\[^14\]  
* Some crews carry filters, but filters do not remove viruses. Purifiers or Micropur are required.  
* Plan water carries carefully for dry camps.

This matters specifically for Santa Claus on June 18 — a dry camp.

Official video: What You Need To Safely Treat Water In The Backcountry — https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GZAiUVfpDuI

---

### **10\. Stove Safety**

The Guidebook emphasizes cooking safety. The Ranger will review:

* Stable stove setup.  
* Closed-toe shoes when cooking.  
* No sitting or kneeling over stoves.  
* Extra water nearby.  
* No unattended hot stove.  
* Safe fuel handling.  
* Avoid burns from boiling water and hot food.

Official video: Choosing The Right Stove For Philmont — https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=agWkvuVDJzc

---

### **11\. Philmont Cooking Method**

Philmont requires crews to cook using the Philmont institutional/expedition-style method, not trail-hiker personal cook systems. The Guidebook is explicit that in-bag rehydration and oven-bag methods are not permitted.\[^15\]

Expect:

* Set up a clean kitchen area inside the Bearmuda Triangle.  
* Boil water.  
* Sanitize dishes and utensils before each meal.  
* Cook or rehydrate food as directed.  
* Serve from the pot.  
* Control all food waste.  
* Use the duty roster so cooking rotates.

Official video: Philmont Backcountry Cooking Method — https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UA\_Q1ZnJDvQ

---

### **12\. Dishwashing, Sump, and Waste**

The Guidebook describes proper dishwashing procedure: scrape and wipe dishes as clean as possible, wash in warm water with mild biodegradable soap, rinse dishes in boiling water, air dry, and sterilize before each meal.\[^16\]

Expect:

* Wash dishes correctly.  
* Strain food particles into the yum-yum bag.  
* Use the sump correctly. Drain waste water through the strainer provided.  
* Carry solid waste to the next staffed camp.  
* Never dump food in the latrine.

Official video: How To Wash Dishes — https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eLt8XjCG7So

---

### **13\. Bear Bags, Smellables, and Wildlife**

The Guidebook to Adventure describes smellables broadly: "Anything with an odor (except the human scent) may attract bears. It does not matter if the odor is food-related."\[^17\]

Smellables include: food, soaps, toothbrushes, toothpaste, lip balm, sunscreen, insect repellent, first aid kits, and any clothing with food odors.

Expect:

* Identify smellables.  
* Keep smellables out of tents.  
* Hang all smellables on bear cables provided at Philmont camps.  
* On neighboring land, the crew will need extra rope for their own system.  
* Bear spray and horns are not allowed at Philmont.

Official videos:  
 How To Hang A Bear Bag — https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DN2y50oUcS8  
 Low Impact Bear Bag Hang — https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4IkonO\_XkPU

---

### **14\. Leave No Trace and Wilderness Pledge**

The Ranger will cover the Wilderness Pledge and its five areas: litter and graffiti, wildlife, water, trails, and campsites.\[^18\]

Expect:

* Camp only where assigned.  
* Stay on route. Do not cut switchbacks.  
* Protect water sources. Never wash in or near springs or streams.  
* Respect staff, camps, and other crews.  
* Complete the conservation project.  
* Leave Philmont better than we found it.

---

### **15\. Ranger Release Standard**

The Ranger will leave when the crew demonstrates reasonable proficiency.

The crew should be able to:

* Wake up and pack efficiently.  
* Follow the route.  
* Move together.  
* Set up camp correctly, including Bearmuda Triangle.  
* Cook safely using the Philmont method.  
* Clean correctly and use the sump.  
* Hang bear bags correctly.  
* Treat water.  
* Manage smellables.  
* Handle basic first aid.  
* Make safe weather decisions.

After Ranger release, mistakes belong to the crew. That is why the first two trail days matter.

---

## **Practice Priorities Before Philmont**

Practice these at home before leaving:

* Pack and unpack in the dark with a headlamp.  
* Cook a Philmont-style dinner as a crew.  
* Wash dishes and strain food waste.  
* Hang bear bags or at least practice the rope and job sequence.  
* Navigate using a paper map and compass.  
* Start hiking early.  
* Break camp under a time target.  
* Rotate crew jobs.  
* Do a short evening debrief after hard training hikes.

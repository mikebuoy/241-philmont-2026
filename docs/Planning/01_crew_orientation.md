## **Daily Light and Sleep Plan**

Most trail mornings will start early.

* **Wake-up target:** about 4:30 AM.  
* **Goal:** be packed and moving about 15 minutes before sunrise.  
* **Why:** cooler hiking, better weather window, more program time, and less stress at camp.  
* **Headlamps:** mostly for morning wake-up, packing, breakfast, and early trail movement. After the first few days, it becomes routine.  
* **Bedtime Target:** When it gets dark \- AKA Hiker’s midnight. This will usually be about 30 min after sunset most nights.

Baldy day and Tooth day will be earlier than normal. We want to be off Baldy and off the Tooth summit area no later than 11:00 AM to reduce exposure to afternoon weather and lightning risk. The Philmont Guidebook to Adventure is direct: "Philmont requires that crews hike during daylight with rare exceptions" and specifically calls out Baldy Mountain and Tooth of Time as popular for early morning starts.\[^1\]

## **Planned vs. Opportunistic Activities**

**Planned activities** are part of our route, our crew plan, or the official itinerary. We expect to do these unless weather, safety, staff availability, or Philmont changes the plan.

**Opportunistic activities** are possible if we arrive with enough time and staff are available. These are not guaranteed. The Crew Leader should ask camp staff what is possible when we arrive.

**Final destination camp** is where we sleep that night. We usually have the best chance of doing the program there.

**Pass-through camp** is a staffed camp we pass during the day. We may get a shorter version of the program if time and staff allow.

**Dry camp** means there is no water at camp. We must carry the water we need for that night and the next morning.

## **Medical Recheck Overview**

We will complete a medical recheck during Stay & Play on Monday, June 15\. Medical recheck is not optional and is not something to work around. It is Philmont's process for confirming every person is physically safe to enter the backcountry.

What will happen:

* Medical forms will be reviewed. Your Annual Health and Medical Record must be complete, signed, and uploaded to Philmont Gateway before arrival.  
* Height, weight, and BMI will be checked against Philmont's required standards.  
* Blood pressure will be checked for all adults.  
* All medications will be reviewed. Every prescription medication must be brought to medical recheck in its original labeled container. After the check, medications can be repacked into your personal container for trail use.  
* Expired medications will not be accepted. This includes EpiPens, inhalers, and any other emergency medication. Expired means not going on trail until it is replaced at your expense.

**Philmont BMI standard:**  
 Maximum BMI of 40 for participants under 21\. Maximum BMI of 32 for participants 21 and older. Anyone over the limit will not be allowed on trail and may be sent home at their own expense. There are no exceptions at check-in.\[^2\]

**Blood pressure standard:**  
 Philmont requires a reading below 160/100 before going on trail. If you are on blood pressure medication, you must be taking it consistently before arrival and throughout the trek. Altitude can cause blood pressure to rise above your normal home reading. Stay on top of your dosing. Stay hydrated — dehydration elevates blood pressure.

This is about your safety and the safety of everyone in your crew who would have to manage a medical emergency in the backcountry.

## **Crew Roles**

These roles are fixed. They are assigned before the trek begins and do not rotate.

**Crew Leader**  
Leads the crew on trail and in camp. Sets the pace, calls breaks, makes route decisions, runs the nightly brief, and owns the Crew Leader copy. The Crew Leader is the primary point of contact for the crew — Scouts go to the Crew Leader first, not advisors.

**Chaplain's Aide**  
Watches the spiritual and emotional health of the crew. Leads chapel and reflective moments. Pays attention to how people are doing, not just how far they hiked. If someone is struggling and won't say so, the Chaplain's Aide is often the first to notice.

**Wilderness Pledge Guía**  
Leads the crew in Leave No Trace practices and the Wilderness Pledge. Keeps the crew accountable for camp cleanliness, water use, trail behavior, and how we treat Philmont's land.

**Lead Advisor**  
Oversees crew safety and logistics from the adult side. Works through the Crew Leader, not around them. The Lead Advisor handles Philmont check-in, the Crew Leader copy, and any escalation required.

**Advisors**  
Support, coach, and protect safety. Do not make decisions the Crew Leader should make. If a Scout has a question or issue, the standard response is: "Ask your Crew Leader."

## **Trail Crew Duties — Duty Roster**

These duties rotate every day. The Crew Leader maintains the roster. No one skips a rotation and no one trades out of a job because they don't feel like it. By the end of the trek, every Scout should be capable of every duty.

**Navigator**  
Reads the map. Keeps the Crew Leader informed of route decisions, upcoming junctions, elevation changes, and water sources. Meets with the Crew Leader and Lead Advisor each night to plan the next day's route.

**Cook**  
Sets up the kitchen inside the Bearmuda Triangle, boils water, prepares the meal using the Philmont cooking method, and serves the crew. Closed-toe shoes required when stoves are on.

**Dishwasher**  
Washes, rinses, and boil-sanitizes all dishes, utensils, and pots after each meal. Manages the sump. Strains food particles into the yum-yum bag. This duty is not optional and is not light work.

**Bear Bag Manager**  
First up in the morning. Brings down the bear bags and oops bag at wake-up. In the evening, organizes all smellables into three piles, loads the bear bags, hangs them on the bear cable, and hangs the oops bag last. Responsible for the site being secure before sleep.

**Water Gatherer**  
Collects water from the source and treats it for the crew. Monitors water supply levels and alerts the Crew Leader when running low. On dry camp days, this duty is the most critical job in camp.

## **Daily Trail Routine**

Our morning start sets the tone for the entire day. An early start means we hike in cooler temperatures, get to activities before other crews, and have real downtime when we arrive at camp. A late start means hiking in the heat of the day, arriving at program camps behind other crews, and missing activities entirely. 

***Every minute of sluggishness in the morning costs us on trail.*** 

**No cooking, no coffee in the morning.** We are moving, not making breakfast. If you need caffeine to function, figure out a cold solution before you leave home. A caffeinated electrolyte packet mixed into your water bottle is a solid option — you get caffeine and hydration at the same time.

**If you are a slow morning person, plan to wake up earlier.** We are working as a team to get up and out together. Do not make the crew wait on you. If you need more time, set your alarm earlier.

**Practice packing up your gear in the dark before you leave home.** Do it with a headlamp, in the same order every time. Organization and repeatable routine are what make a 20-minute pack-up possible. *If the first time you do this in the dark is at Philmont, you will be the one slowing the crew down.*

### **Morning Trail Routine**

Here is what a normal trail morning looks like. This becomes faster as the crew gets better at it. By Day 5 it should feel almost automatic.

**4:30 AM — Wake-up**

* Crew Leader and Bear Bag crew are first to wake and rise.   
* Everyone gets up — no negotiating

**4:30–4:50 AM — Pack and break camp**

* Change into hiking clothes inside your tent  
* Pack sleeping bag, sleep pad, camp clothes, and all personal items — still inside the tent  
* Get out, pack up your tent with your tent buddy  
* Bear bags come down   
* Get your smellables from the bear bag, finish packing your pack  
* Stage your breakfast food somewhere easy to reach while hiking — top pocket or hip belt pocket  
* Stack your pack in the pack line  
* Help your crewmates  
* Walk the campsite for Leave No Trace — nothing left behind, no trace you were there

**4:50–5:00 AM — Packline & Final check**

* Wilderness Guia walks the campsite  
* Nothing left behind, sump covered if required  
* Pack in the pack line and camp clear — you can start eating breakfast items while you wait

**\~5:00–5:10 AM — Hike On\!**

* Goal is to move when it is light enough to see our shoelaces — we’ll watch sunrise from the trail  
* Breakfast comes with you — eat on trail or at the first 20-minute break  
* Be bold and start cold — Our first steps may feel cold and stiff. Keep moving and you will warm up fast

### **Arriving at Staffed Camps**

When we arrive at a staffed camp — whether it is our destination for the night or a pass-through — there is a standard sequence. Do not scatter. The crew moves together and follows this order.

1. **Check in with staff.**  
The Crew Leader checks in at the staff shelter or program area. Staff will confirm our arrival, direct us to our campsite, and tell us what programs are available and when. Do not start setting up camp before check-in. **Activity sign-ups are first come, first served.** Check in promptly so we are not at the back of the line.

2. **Establish the pack line.**  
Stack all packs together in the designated area. The Crew Leader counts packs and confirms everyone arrived. Packs stay on the pack line — not scattered across the campsite.

3. **Porch talks.**  
Many staffed camps offer informal talks from staff on the camp's history, the program, the surrounding terrain, or Philmont culture. Attend these. They add real context to the trek and are often one of the best parts of the day. Stay engaged and ask questions.

4. **Camp program.**  
Participate in the scheduled program. Programs are the point of staffed camps — do not skip them or arrive so late that the crew misses out.

5. **Hang bear bags.**  
Once campsite is assigned, get smellables organized and bear bags hung before cooking or tents. This is always the first camp task.

**Note on pass-throughs and flexible timing:** At some camps we will do the program before we reach our final destination camp for the night, or the program runs while we are still hiking through. Be ready to hang bear bags mid-hike if a pass-through camp requires it, or to pivot to bear bags immediately after a program before continuing. Stay flexible and follow staff direction.

### **Campsite Routine**

Evening camp routine follows a similar pattern. When we arrive at camp, crew gear comes before personal gear. The official Philmont order verified against Ranger training:

#### **Establish Campsite**

1. **Pack line.** Stack all packs together in the designated area. Crew Leader counts packs and confirms everyone arrived.

2. **Identify the Bearmuda Triangle.** Find the fire ring, bear cable, and sump — these form the three points of the triangle. All smellable items stay inside this zone for the entire stay. Tents go at least 50 feet outside it.

3. **Hang bear bags — first priority.** Sort gear into three piles near the fire ring:
   - Food
   - Crew gear (dining fly, stoves, pots, bear ropes, toilet paper)
   - Personal smellables (toiletries, medications, chapstick, sunscreen, etc.)  
   Hang bear bags before setting up tents or cooking. This is the first priority at camp, not the last.

4. **Set up the dining fly (tarp).** Inside the Bearmuda Triangle, near the fire ring. Check the four W's: Wind (corner facing into wind for stability), Water (avoid drainage lines), Wildlife (avoid game trails and ant hills), Widow Makers (no dead trees or branches overhead).

5. **Set up tents.** At least 50 feet from the Bearmuda Triangle. Cluster tents 5–7 feet apart. No isolated tents. Packs are smellable — keep them in the Bearmuda Triangle or covered outside, not piled under the tarp. Everyone puts their pack cover on and leans their pack against a tree in the fire ring area.

#### **Dinner**

6. **Cook dinner.** Cooks start water near the fire ring. Only cooks inside the kitchen area. Closed-toe shoes required when stoves are on. Watch for steam burns when opening lids and handling boiling water — steam burns happen fast and hurt. Never hand a pot of boiling water across the fire ring or over another person.

7. **Eat dinner.** Eat all of it. Every calorie matters at altitude.

8. **Lick your bowls clean.** Then take your turn as the human sump for the food pot — someone eats whatever is left in the pot before the dishwasher starts cleanup. Waste nothing.

9. **Dishwasher cleans up.** Wash, rinse, boil-sanitize all dishes, utensils, and pots. Strain food particles into the yum-yum bag. Use the sump.

#### **Next Day Planning**

10. **Crew Leader briefs with Navigator and Lead Advisor.** Map out tomorrow's route. Identify water sources, navigation decision points, and timing.  
11. **Crew Leader runs the nightly crew brief.** Route, distance, elevation, water, program timing, food pickup if applicable, and wake-up time.

#### **Hang Opps Bag**

12. **Everyone checks pockets and pack for smellables.** Double-check the site.  
13. **Oops bag hung.** All remaining smellables in the oops bag — food, first aid kit, toiletries, smellable water bottles — hung on the bear cable.  
14. **In tents before Dark when possible.** Get your well deserved sleep. Your 4:30am wake up call comes early. Don’t stay up late.

## **Altitude — What to Expect**

Cimarron, NM sits at about 6,400 feet. The Philmont backcountry runs from about 6,500 to 12,441 feet. Most of us are coming from near sea level. Your body will notice.

**Acute Mountain Sickness (AMS)**  
 AMS can hit anyone regardless of fitness. Being in shape does not prevent it. AMS is caused by the reduced oxygen at altitude, not by how hard you trained.

Common symptoms:

* Headache, especially at the back or sides of the skull  
* Nausea or upset stomach  
* Fatigue that feels out of proportion to what you did  
* Poor sleep, even when exhausted  
* Lightheadedness or dizziness  
* Difficulty concentrating or irritability

AMS usually appears in the first 2–3 days and improves as your body adjusts. Mild symptoms are normal. Severe or worsening symptoms need to be reported to advisors immediately.

**What helps:**

* Hydrate aggressively. 6–8 quarts of water per day is the Philmont target. This is far more than you drink at home. Your body loses fluid fast at altitude even when you don't feel like you're sweating.  
* Eat full meals even when you don't feel hungry. Your body needs the calories to function and adjust.  
* Sleep as well as you can. The first few nights at altitude, sleep quality is often worse than usual. This is normal.  
* Avoid overexertion on the first 2–3 days. The short mileage on Day 1 is intentional.  
* If symptoms worsen or include severe headache, confusion, loss of coordination, or shortness of breath at rest, tell an advisor immediately. These can be signs of serious AMS that requires descent.

**Hydration target: 6–8 quarts of water per day on trail.** That is roughly 1.5 to 2 liters every few hours. Drink before you feel thirsty. By the time you feel thirsty, you are already behind.

**The altitude also affects sleep, mood, and appetite for the first 2–3 days.** If someone in the crew seems unusually irritable, flat, or off, check in with them. It is likely altitude doing its work, not a personal problem.

## **Lightning and Weather — What Every Scout Needs to Know**

Afternoon thunderstorms are common at Philmont from late June through early August. Storms can build in less than an hour. There is no warning system in the backcountry other than your own eyes.

**What to watch for:**

* Cumulus clouds building rapidly, especially over peaks and ridges, starting mid-morning.  
* Darkening sky to the west or southwest.  
* Distant thunder. If you hear thunder, lightning is already close enough to be dangerous.  
* Your hair standing up, skin tingling, or metal gear buzzing. Get off the ridge immediately.

**The crew rules:**

* Off Baldy summit by 11:00 AM, no later than 12:00.  
* Off the Tooth summit and exposed ridge by 11:00 AM, no later than 12:00.  
* No exceptions. No "it looks like it's clearing." Move.

**If you are caught in lightning:**

* Get off exposed ridges, peaks, and open ground.  
* Get away from lone trees, tall objects, wire fences, and metal equipment.  
* Do not shelter under a cliff or in a shallow cave — ground current can travel through rock.  
* Spread the crew out at least 30 feet apart so one strike does not take out multiple people.  
* Lightning position: crouch low on the balls of your feet, feet together, hands over ears. Do not lie flat. Do not sit on the ground. Minimize contact.  
* If someone is struck, they need first aid immediately. A lightning victim is not carrying a charge — it is safe to touch them.

**The simple rule:** if you can hear thunder, you are in the danger zone. Move to lower elevation and away from exposed terrain.

## **Foot Care**

Blisters are the most common reason Scouts slow down or struggle at Philmont. They are almost entirely preventable.

#### **Before the trek:**

* Your boots or trail runners must be broken in. If your footwear is new, it is not ready. Start wearing them now. Both boots and trail runners work at Philmont — whatever you choose, they must be comfortable and already molded to your feet.  
* Toenails cut short and square, not rounded at the corners. Do this a week before you leave, not the night before.  
* Address any existing foot issues — ingrown nails, calluses — before you arrive.

#### **On trail:**

* Change socks daily. Wet socks cause blisters faster than anything else.  
* At any rest stop, if part of your foot feels hot or rubbing, stop and deal with it before it becomes a blister. A hot spot treated immediately takes 30 seconds. A blister that develops costs you the rest of the day and several trail days after.  
* Treatment: moleskin, medical tape, or both. Surround the blister with moleskin cut in a donut shape. Do not pop blisters unless they are under high pressure — once opened, they are prone to infection.  
* If a blister has already formed, reduce friction and protect it. Change shoes if possible.

**Pack target weight:**  
 Philmont recommends keeping pack weight to 20-5% of your body weight for most participants including food and water. Heavier than that and your knees and feet pay for it over 11 days. This is why shakedown matters. Extra gear is not safety — it is weight.

## **The Burro**

On Day 7 (June 22\) we pick up a burro at the Miranda Burro Pens and hike with it to Pueblano. We drop the burro off at Ponil on Day 8 (June 23).

**What to expect:**

* The burro is a working animal, not a pet and not entertainment. Treat it with calm, consistent handling.  
* Burros are stubborn by design. They will stop. They will resist. They will test you. This is not a malfunction, it's a built-in feature.   
* Staff at Miranda will show us how to load the burro, how to lead it, and what it can and cannot carry. Follow their instructions exactly.  
* The burro carries some of our gear, which means loading and securing it correctly matters. Gear shifting on the burro is a problem on trail.  
* Keep the burro moving at a steady pace. The crew's pace adjusts to the burro, not the other way around.  
* Assign handlers in rotation so no one person manages the burro the entire day.  
* Do not let the burro eat along the trail without staff guidance.

*The burro will slow us down relative to a normal hiking day. Plan for it. Do not let burro frustration become crew frustration.*

The burro is also guaranteed to produce some of the best stories of the trip. Somewhere between Miranda and Pueblano, the crew will discover who the "Burro Whisperer" is. It might be the Scout who makes eye contact, speaks calmly, and actually gets the animal moving. It might be whoever is just stubborn enough to out-stubborn a burro. It might be the person who figures out that the burro responds to a very specific trail snack. No one knows ahead of time. The burro decides.

*A few things that have been said to burros on Philmont trails and did not work: "Come on buddy," "I will carry you," "This is not how I wanted to spend Thursday," and "Please."*

Plan for the pace. Laugh when it stops. Name it something good. These are the days that become the stories.

Some programs charge for additional rounds. Bring small bills — cash only at ranges.

## **Electronics and Devices**

There is no electricity in the backcountry. No outlets, no charging stations, no exceptions.

If you bring a phone, a GPS device, or a camera, you are responsible for powering it. A small battery pack is your only option. Plan your charge strategy before you leave home and make sure your battery pack is full when we step off the bus at the trailhead.

**Phone use on trail is limited to:**
- Offline maps and navigation
- Photos and video
- Planning reference (this document, trail notes)
- A text or call if we happen to hit a rare pocket of service

Put your phone in airplane mode at the trailhead and expect it to stay there. If you set that expectation for yourself now, you will not spend 11 days frustrated that you cannot check anything. Airplane mode also saves your battery for the things that actually matter out there.

**Keep it in perspective.** You are on one of the best treks in Scouting. The mountains do not need a filter. Take pictures. Put the phone away. Be here.

## **The Arrowhead Award**

The Philmont Arrowhead is the award for completing a trek and meeting Philmont's trail standards. It is not automatic. Requirements include:

* Completing the trek.  
* Completing the assigned conservation project.  
* Following the Wilderness Pledge throughout the trek.  
* Maintaining Leave No Trace practices.  
* No graffiti. No shortcuts. No mistreatment of the land or facilities.

For Trek 12-23, our conservation project is new trail construction at Sioux on Day 8 (June 23\) at 2:00 PM. Missing conservation means missing the Arrowhead.

The Arrowhead is one of the things that makes a Philmont trek different from any other backpacking trip. It is worth earning correctly.

01Know Your Pack Weight Limit
What is base weight? Base weight is everything in your pack before food, water, and crew gear. It is everything on your personal packing list — sleeping bag, pad, clothing, rain gear, personal items. Your shelter (tent or tent components) is part of your base weight. Food, water, and crew gear are added on top. The combined total is your pack weight on the trail.

On Day 2 and Day 8 — your two heaviest start days — every crew member carries their base weight plus 9–11 lbs of crew gear and food. That drops by ~1.5 lbs per person per day as food is consumed. By Day 11 at Clarks Fork, food weight is down to ~1.5 lbs per person. Crew gear distribution at HQ will account for body weight — lighter crew members carry lighter items.

Philmont recommends total pack weight not exceed 25% of your body weight. Find your row below.

03Water

We carry 4 filters total: 2 Platypus QuickDraw + 2 Sawyer Squeeze. Four independent filter paths for fast bottle filling at any water source. Micropur tabs carried as a supplemental treatment option.

Personal water carry: Carry 1–2L while hiking. Carry up to 2 additional liters for dry stretches or the Santa Claus dry camp. At 1L ≈ 2.2 lbs. Top off at every reliable source — do not wait until you are low.

Hydration is a skill — train for it now. Philmont's dry desert climate and elevation demand significantly more water intake than most crew members are used to. On trail you need to drink 3–4 liters of water per day minimum, alternating with sports drinks to replace electrolytes lost during strenuous hiking. A good rule of thumb on trail: take several sips every 15–20 minutes — do not wait until you are thirsty. Thirst is a late sign of dehydration.

Start building this habit now. If you are not used to drinking 3–4 liters a day, your body will resist it at first. Practice on your training hikes. Dehydration is one of Philmont's most common causes of illness, and it is entirely preventable.

07Cooking Method & Cleanup

Standard Philmont method

Philmont meals are designed around a boil-and-rehydrate system. Water is boiled, poured into the food, and the meal rehydrates in the issued pot. All utensils and bowls are sterilized in boiling water before eating. Cleanup follows strict Leave No Trace protocol — all grey water goes into a sump hole, never on the ground surface or near a water source.

Our hybrid method — HE pot + issued pot

We run two cook teams simultaneously. Each team uses a Fire Maple FMS-118 stove with a Bulin heat exchange pot to quick-boil water, then transfers to the issued 8-quart pot for sterilizing, rehydrating, serving, and cleanup. This cuts boil time 20–40% at altitude and conserves fuel across the trek.

1
Boil
Fill HE pot with water. Ignite Fire Maple stove. Bring to full rolling boil — HE pot reaches boil significantly faster than standard pots at altitude.
2
Sterilize bowls & utensils
Using the pot lifter or multi-tool, completely submerge all personal bowls and eating utensils in the boiling water. Hold for several seconds. This is the official Philmont method and is required before every meal to prevent foodborne illness on the trail.
3
Rehydrate & serve
Transfer boiled water to the issued 8-quart pot. Add food. Cover and let rehydrate per meal instructions (typically 10–15 minutes). Serve from the issued pot into sterilized personal bowls. Only open and rehydrate the food you intend to eat.
4
Eat clean
Eat everything in your bowl. Lick it clean. Every bit of food left behind becomes a smellable. Encourage crew members to take only what they can eat.
5
Wash & rinse
Add a small amount of water and one drop of camp suds to the issued pot. Scrub with the issued scrubby. Rinse personal bowls, utensils, and cooking pots until free of food residue and soap.
6
Sump & dispose
Pour all grey water — wash and rinse water — through the strainer into the sump hole. Strain food particles into the yum yum bag for disposal at the next staffed camp. Never pour grey water on the ground surface or near any water source.
7
Bear bag
All food bags, wrappers, and smellables go into bear bags immediately after cleanup. Bear bags are hung before the crew goes to sleep — no exceptions.
Cook team structure: 2–3 crew members run both stoves, both pots, and cleanup after each meal. The crew leader assigns cook and cleanup roles. Rotate crew members across cook teams so everyone learns the system by Day 3.
08Bear Bags & Smellables

Philmont requires all smellables stored in bear bags hung from a tree each night — no exceptions. This is not a suggestion. Ranger staff will check. A smellable is anything with a scent that could attract wildlife.

Everything below goes in the bear bag at night:

All food and snacks
Toothpaste and floss
Sunscreen and lip balm
Insect repellent
Deodorant
Soap and hand sanitizer
Trash and food wrappers
Chapstick
Scented lotion or wipes
Any scented medication
Empty food bags
Gum and candy
If it has a scent, it goes in the bag. Items left in tents or packs overnight are a Philmont violation and a safety risk for the entire crew.
09Footwear

Boots

Boots are the recommended footwear for this itinerary. 81 miles over 11 days with significant elevation change demands a supportive, broken-in boot. Ankle support matters on descent — Day 6 drops 5,820 ft and Day 12 drops 6,870 ft.

Break in your boots before you arrive. 20–30 miles minimum on varied terrain before June 16. New or barely worn boots will produce blisters within the first two days. If you are wearing new boots to Philmont, you are choosing blisters and potentially compromising the crew.

Trail runners

Trail runners are viable for experienced hikers with ultralight kits and strong ankles. Not recommended for first-time Philmont hikers or anyone carrying over 30 lbs total. The tradeoff is ankle support — on loose trail at elevation, a rolled ankle ends your trek. If you choose trail runners, they must have aggressive tread and be fully broken in. Accept the stability tradeoff consciously.

10Preparing at Home

You are leaving from approximately 1,000 ft elevation and arriving at Philmont's base at 6,500 ft, with trail camps between 7,100 and 9,700 ft and Baldy summit at 12,441 ft on Day 6. Your body cannot fully acclimatize to altitude in advance at home — but it can arrive physically prepared to handle the workload. Here is your window:

May 16
Shakedown
Pack shakedown hike
Load your full pack at or near trek weight. Hike with the crew. Identify fit issues, hot spots on feet, and gear that doesn't work before it matters. Weigh every pack. Anyone over their 25% ceiling needs to cut weight this weekend.
May 17 –
June 7
Loaded hikes — progressive distance and weight
Hike with your actual pack at trek weight on varied terrain. Aim for 2–3 hikes per week, increasing distance. If you have access to elevation gain — hills, stairs, stadium steps — use it. Minimum target: 3–5 miles per session with a loaded pack. The goal is time on feet with weight, not speed.
Now –
June 14
Hydration training — start today
Begin drinking 3–4 liters of water per day now. Alternate with electrolyte drinks on training days. Your body needs to adapt to this volume — it will feel like a lot at first. This is one of the most impactful things you can do to prepare for Philmont. Dehydration is the most common preventable illness on trek.
Now –
June 14
Boot mileage
If your boots are not broken in yet, start now. 20–30 miles minimum before June 16 shakedown. Wear your trail socks. Walk on varied terrain. Break in the boots you are actually bringing — not a different pair.
June 8 –
June 13
Taper — rest and recover
Reduce mileage the week before departure. Let your body recover. Focus on sleep, hydration, and nutrition. Do not do a long hard hike 2–3 days before you fly out. Arrive rested, not depleted.
June 14
Fly day — hydrate on the plane
Airplane cabin air is extremely dry. Drink water throughout the flight. Avoid alcohol. Arrive at Philmont already ahead on hydration. You will feel the altitude from the moment you land at 6,500 ft — being well-hydrated before you step off the plane makes a real difference on Day 1.
Altitude reality check: Living at 1,000 ft means your body is not adapted to reduced oxygen at elevation. You will feel more breathless on uphills than you expect, especially Days 3–6 as you climb into Baldy country. This is normal. The best response is to slow your pace, breathe steadily, drink water, and eat consistently. Do not race the crew on the uphills. A sustainable pace gets everyone to the summit. A fast pace gets one person there and the rest struggling behind.
11Altitude
⚑ This trek reaches 12,441 ft on Day 6 — Baldy Mountain. Most of the trek runs between 7,000 and 10,000 ft. If you live and train at low elevation, your body will feel the difference immediately. Altitude reduces oxygen availability — your heart and lungs work harder at the same effort level. A heavy pack accelerates this significantly.

Acute Mountain Sickness (AMS) — headache, nausea, fatigue, dizziness — can affect anyone regardless of fitness level. It is not a toughness issue. The best defenses: arrive hydrated, keep your pack weight in check, hike at a sustainable pace, eat and drink consistently. If symptoms develop, tell the crew leader or an advisor immediately. Do not push through and go silent.
